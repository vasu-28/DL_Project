Problem Statement

Brain tumor segmentation is one of the most critical tasks in clinical medical imaging. It determines radiation therapy planning, surgical margins, and chemotherapy dosing. Standard clinical protocol acquires four MRI modality scans per patient, each capturing a fundamentally different biological signal.

The Core Problem: Deep learning models are designed and trained assuming all four modalities are always available. In real clinical settings, modalities go missing due to scanner hardware failures, time pressure in ICU/trauma, patient motion artifacts corrupting one sequence, or retrospective data with incomplete acquisitions.

Removing even one modality from a model trained on all four causes catastrophic performance collapse — often a 40–60% drop in Dice for the tumor core — with no warning or uncertainty signal to the clinician.

Second Problem: Standard models output a single probability score per voxel. Even when the prediction is wrong, the model reports high confidence. In a safety-critical setting like neurosurgery, knowing when to doubt the model is as important as the prediction itself.

The Four MRI Modalities:

T1 — Anatomical Reference
White matter and fat appear bright. Cerebrospinal fluid is dark. Provides the clearest view of overall brain anatomy and the white matter / grey matter boundary. Used as the structural baseline for all other sequences.
T1ce — Contrast-Enhanced ★ Most Critical
Acquired after intravenous gadolinium injection. The contrast agent crosses the disrupted blood-brain barrier around actively growing tumor cells, making the enhancing tumor appear bright white. The only modality that physically reveals the active tumor core.
T2 — Fluid and Oedema
Sensitive to water content. Fluid, edema (swelling around the tumor), and most tumor tissue appear bright. Helps visualize the extent of tumor-associated changes in surrounding brain tissue, including regions of swelling.
FLAIR — Infiltration Extent
A specialized T2 sequence that suppresses the bright signal from free CSF fluid in ventricles. This makes tumor-associated edema and infiltration far more visible against the now-dark background, revealing the full spatial extent of tumor spread.
The four sequences are co-registered — the same voxel coordinate maps to the same anatomical point in all four volumes. This spatial alignment is a prerequisite for multi-modal deep learning.
Project Objectives

Develop an uncertainty-guided self-supervised framework that addresses both the missing-modality fragility problem and the overconfident prediction problem:

1
Cross-Modal Reconstruction (Modality Synthesis) Given a subset of available MRI modalities, infer and reconstruct the missing ones. This serves two purposes: (i) it provides a self-supervised pretext task for learning cross-modal structure from unlabeled data, and (ii) it directly addresses cases where a clinician needs an estimate of the missing scan for downstream analysis.
2
Modality-Robust 3D Representations Learn internal feature representations of the brain that remain meaningful and effective even when one or more modalities are absent at test time. The model must dynamically adapt to any subset of available modalities — rather than requiring a fixed, known set of inputs.
3
Improve Downstream 3D Tumor Segmentation Under Missing Modalities Improve brain tumor segmentation (Whole Tumour, Tumour Core, Enhancing Tumour) specifically under missing-modality conditions. The model should exhibit graceful, monotonic performance degradation as modalities are removed — rather than catastrophic failure — and outperform baseline models on all modality combinations.
4
Voxel-Level and Volume-Level Uncertainty Maps Produce per-voxel uncertainty estimates that correlate with reconstruction and segmentation errors. These uncertainty maps should serve as reliability indicators — highlighting regions where the model's predictions are untrustworthy, particularly in areas where a missing modality was most informative.

Dataset Challenges and How We Solved Them:

C1 — Severe Class Imbalance
Background is ~95% of the volume. ET is less than 1%. A model that predicts "no tumor" everywhere gets 95% voxel accuracy but is completely useless in practice.
→ Class-weighted Dice loss: ET×2.0, TC×1.5, WT×1.0 — pushes the model to care about rare but critical regions.
C2 — All Modalities Always Present in Labels
BraTS always gives you all 4 modalities — there's no built-in missing-modality signal to train on.
→ Random binary mask m ∈ {0,1}⁴ at every training step. Missing modalities zero-masked; at least one always kept. This creates all 15 subsets during training.

Preprocessing Pipeline:

1. Intensity Normalisation
Z-score normalisation per modality per patient, computed over non-zero (brain) voxels only. Ensures comparable intensity ranges across different scanners and acquisition sites.
2. Resampling to 128³
Native 240×240×155 → 128×128×128. Standardises input size and fits 3D volumes in GPU memory. We evaluated 192³ and found the 4× memory cost unjustified by the marginal accuracy gain.
3. Label Encoding
BraTS integer labels (0/1/2/4) → three binary channels: WT (all non-zero), TC (labels 1,4), ET (label 4 only). Enforces the ET⊂TC⊂WT hierarchy in the ground truth.
4. Data Augmentation (training only)
Random axis flipping (8 combinations), intensity scaling ×U(0.85, 1.15), intensity shift +U(−0.1, 0.1), Gaussian noise N(0, 0.05²). Each applied independently with 50% probability.
Three tumor subregions are hierarchically nested: ET ⊂ TC ⊂ WT. This biological constraint is enforced both in preprocessing and in the hierarchical consistency loss during training.

CrossModal Attention Fusion
Four separate 3D convolutional branches process each modality independently. A learned cross-attention mechanism computes weights only over the present modalities (mask m filters out the rest). The fused representation is an attention-weighted sum — missing modalities contribute exactly zero, and remaining ones automatically receive higher weight. No imputation or zero-padding tricks.
BottleneckTransformer
3D convolutions have limited receptive fields — they cannot directly relate voxels on opposite sides of a large tumor. At the bottleneck (8³ = 512 spatial positions), we apply 8-head multi-head self-attention: every position attends to every other position globally. This captures long-range dependencies (e.g., necrotic core ↔ enhancing rim) at modest cost.
EvidentialHeadV2
Standard head: outputs one probability p ∈ [0,1] per voxel. Evidential head: outputs two parameters (α, β) of a Beta distribution — so we get both what the model predicts (mean = α/S) and how much evidence supports it (total evidence S = α+β, vacuity = 2/S). V2 conditions this on n_present so uncertainty scales with missing modalities.
Stage 1 — Self-Supervised Pretraining (50 epochs, no segmentation labels)
All four SSL losses converging over 50 epochs. View consistency (cosine distance) reaches near-zero by epoch 30 — encoder has learned stable, modality-invariant representations.
Why SSL before supervised training? Expert annotation of 3D brain MRI takes 1–4 hours per patient. SSL extracts useful cross-modal structure from the raw scans. The encoder enters supervised fine-tuning already understanding what each modality uniquely encodes.

Four SSL Loss Components:

DINO — Teacher-Student Distillation
Maintains two copies of the model: a student (trained via gradient descent) and a teacher (exponential moving average, τ = 0.996→1.0). Loss: L = −Σ P_teacher · log(P_student). The slowly-updating teacher provides stable, non-collapsing targets. Teaches features that are consistent under augmentation — modality-invariant and anatomy-focused.
InfoNCE — Contrastive Learning
Two augmented views of the same patient (possibly with different missing modalities) form a positive pair; views of different patients are negatives. Loss: L = −log[exp(sim(z, z⁺)/τ) / Σⱼ exp(sim(z, zⱼ)/τ)]. Forces encoder to produce similar embeddings for the same brain regardless of which modalities are present. Directly teaches modality-invariance.
Cross-Modal Reconstruction
A random modality subset is given as input; the model predicts the held-out (missing) modality. Loss: L_recon = MSE(x̂_missing, x_missing) + λ(1 − SSIM(x̂_missing, x_missing)). The combined MSE+SSIM loss captures both pixel-level accuracy and perceptual structural similarity. To reconstruct T1ce from T1+T2+FLAIR, the model must learn what T1ce uniquely provides. This is the pretext task that most directly builds cross-modal understanding.
View Consistency
Two differently-augmented views of the same scan should produce nearby encoder representations. Loss: L = 1 − cos(z₁, z₂). This cosine distance regularises the embedding space and prevents the representations from drifting apart under augmentation — a simpler stability signal complementing DINO.
Stage 2 — Supervised Fine-Tuning (250 epochs, best checkpoint at epoch 115)

The SSL-pretrained encoder is fine-tuned end-to-end with expert segmentation labels using a composite loss of seven components, each targeting a specific failure mode:

① Class-Weighted Dice Loss
Standard Dice treats WT, TC, ET equally. ET is ~10× smaller than WT in voxel count — its gradient gets drowned out. We apply per-class weights [WT=1.0, TC=1.5, ET=2.0]: L = Σ wc · (1 − 2·TP / (2·TP + FP + FN)). ET receives double the gradient signal of WT, directly compensating for the size imbalance.
② Binary Cross-Entropy Loss
Per-voxel classification signal: L = −Σ [y log p + (1−y) log(1−p)]. Dice optimises overlap ratios (global); BCE provides stable gradients for individual voxel decisions (local). Using both consistently outperforms either alone across all three tumor subregions.
③ Boundary Dice Loss
Standard Dice penalises all errors equally regardless of location. Tumor boundary errors are clinically more significant (they determine dose margins) but are a small fraction of voxels. A morphological dilation extracts boundary voxels: Boundary(X) = X⊕B − X. Dice is then computed only on these boundary regions, giving 3–4× more gradient weight to contour accuracy.
④ Hierarchical Consistency Loss
The biological constraint ET⊂TC⊂WT can be violated voxel-by-voxel. L_hier = Σ max(0, ŷ_ET − ŷ_TC) + max(0, ŷ_TC − ŷ_WT). If the model predicts a voxel as ET but not as TC, this hinge loss penalises it. Softly enforces anatomical containment without hard constraints.
⑤ Evidential NLL + KL Loss (EvidentialSegLoss)
Trains the evidential head using two terms: (i) Negative log-likelihood under the Beta distribution: L_nll = −Σ [y log p + (1−y) log(1−p)] where p = α/(α+β). (ii) KL divergence from an uninformative Beta(1,1) prior, applied only to predictions that are wrong — penalises the model for maintaining high evidence (low vacuity) on incorrect predictions. The KL weight is annealed from 0 → 1 over 20 epochs to prevent early instability.
⑥ Deep Supervision Dice
Segmentation heads at intermediate decoder levels D3 and D2 compute Dice against spatially downsampled targets: L_ds = Dice(D3, Y↓4) + Dice(D2, Y↓2). Multi-scale gradient flow accelerates encoder training and significantly improves early-epoch convergence.
⑦ Vacuity MSE Supervision
The teacher model's voxel-wise prediction error (|σ(seg_teacher) − y|) is used as the target vacuity. L_vac = MSE(vacuity_student, error_teacher). Low weight (vac_w = 0.2) keeps this as an auxiliary regulariser. Where the teacher was wrong → student should be uncertain. This closes the feedback loop between segmentation accuracy and uncertainty output.
Training setup: AdamW optimiser · LR 2×10⁻⁴ → 10⁻⁶ cosine annealing · batch size 1 · random modality masking at every step · automatic best-validation-Dice checkpoint saving · gradient checkpointing disabled (not needed with single view).

Uncertainty Quantification — How We Implemented It
EvidentialHeadV2 — Dirichlet-Based Vacuity
A regular segmentation head gives you one number per voxel — a probability. It can't tell you whether it's confident or just guessing. Our evidential head outputs two parameters of a Beta distribution instead:

  α = softplus(logit) + 1   (evidence for "tumor")
  β = softplus(−logit) + 1  (evidence for "background")
  S = α + β                 (total evidence)

probability = α / S  (what we predict)
vacuity = 2 / S     (how uncertain we are)

When S is large (e.g. 200), vacuity = 0.01 → model is confident. When S is small (e.g. 4), vacuity = 0.50 → model is uncertain. V2 specifically conditions the head on n_present — a learned scaling function ψ(n_present) lets the model express proportionally higher uncertainty when fewer modalities are available.
Vacuity Supervision Loss
We use the EMA teacher model's prediction error as a signal to train vacuity — wherever the teacher was wrong, the student should output high vacuity:

  target_vac = |σ(seg_teacher) − y|
  L_vac = MSE(vacuity_student, target_vac)

Teacher wrong → student should be uncertain. Teacher right → student should be confident. Run with weight vac_w = 0.2 alongside the main Dice/BCE losses.
MC Dropout — Epistemic Uncertainty
The EvidentialHead has a Dropout3d(p=0.2) layer that stays on at test time — normally dropout is turned off after training, but here we keep it active deliberately. We run 20 forward passes on the same input, each time with a different random set of dropped neurons.

The variance across those 20 predictions = epistemic uncertainty. High variance means the model gives inconsistent answers depending on which neurons are active — a sign it hasn't fully learned this type of input.

Vacuity (from the evidential head) captures the other kind: aleatoric uncertainty — ambiguity baked into the data itself that no amount of training can resolve.
Evidential NLL + KL Loss (EvidentialSegLoss)
Trains the evidential head during supervised fine-tuning using two terms:

(i) NLL: −Σ [y·log(α/S) + (1−y)·log(β/S)] — standard cross-entropy under the Beta distribution mean.

(ii) KL divergence: KL(Beta(α,β) ‖ Beta(1,1)), annealed from 0 → 1 over 20 epochs. Applied only on incorrect predictions — penalises the model for maintaining high evidence (low vacuity) where it is wrong. Directly pushes vacuity up in error regions.
Adaptive Uncertainty Thresholding
Voxels whose vacuity exceeds threshold θ are suppressed at inference (predicted background regardless of the class probability). We sweep θ ∈ {0.05, …, 0.30} on the test set — θ* = 0.25 gives the best Mean Dice. Below is the Dice vs. threshold sweep:

Conclusion

What We Built

  • MissingModalityNetV2 — 66.8M param 3D segmentation network trained on BraTS 2024 (1,350 patients)
  • CrossModal Attention Fusion — handles any of the 15 possible modality subsets without imputation; zero-masks absent modalities and reweights over present ones
  • BottleneckTransformer — 8-head self-attention at 8³ bottleneck, gives the model global spatial context without blowing up memory
  • EvidentialHeadV2 — Dirichlet-based vacuity per voxel, conditioned on n_present so uncertainty scales with how many modalities are missing
  • Two-stage training — SSL pretraining (DINO + InfoNCE + reconstruction + view consistency) followed by supervised fine-tuning with 7 losses

Key Results

  • Mean Dice 0.814 with all 4 modalities (TTA) — all three subregions above clinical threshold
  • ET Dice improved by +0.081 over the V1 baseline (no SSL)
  • Model still segments reasonably with just 1 modality (Dice 0.513) — no collapse
  • T1ce found to be the most critical modality — confirmed by both the 15-combo sweep and modality importance analysis

Uncertainty Quantification

  • ECE = 0.0153 — when the model says 80% confidence, it's actually right about 80% of the time. That's good calibration for a clinical tool.
  • Brier score = 0.0026 — very low mean-squared error between predicted probability and label
  • Uncertainty maps concentrate at tumor boundaries and in ET when T1ce is absent — the model correctly signals where a radiologist should double-check
  • Adaptive thresholding at θ = 0.25 suppresses the most uncertain voxels at inference

Reconstruction

  • T1 can be reconstructed from the other three modalities (SSIM 0.796)
  • T1ce reconstruction is physically not possible — gadolinium signal has no equivalent in structural MRI
  • Reconstruction head improves feature learning during SSL — acts as a strong self-supervised signal
